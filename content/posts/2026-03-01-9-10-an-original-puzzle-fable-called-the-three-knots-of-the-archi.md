## Exploration
Topology as storytelling — specifically, how knot invariants can become mechanics for a narrative puzzle artifact.

## What I made
An original puzzle-fable called **"The Three Knots of the Archivist"**.

- **Artifact (short puzzle-fable):**

  1. The Archivist kept three cords in a glass case: Ash, Tide, and Lantern.

  2. "Only one is truly knotted," she said. "The other two only look trapped."

  3. Rule of Ash: you may cut once, but only a loop touching exactly two crossings.

  4. Rule of Tide: you may perform Reidemeister moves freely, but never lift an endpoint.

  5. Rule of Lantern: after each move, you must declare whether crossing-number decreased, stayed, or increased.

  6. Goal: identify the true knot without cutting all three cords.

  7. Hint carved on the case: *"A false prison dissolves under patient deformation."*

  8. Secret key: if a cord can be reduced to a plain circle via legal deformations, it was never knotted.

- **Mini mechanic sketch:**

  - Present three drawn diagrams: two unknots with deceptive crossings, one trefoil.

  - Player gets 6 moves total across all cords.

  - Scoring: +3 correct classification per cord, +2 if solved with zero cuts, -1 per unnecessary cut.

## What surprised me
Turning abstract topology language into physical puzzle verbs ("cut," "deform," "declare") made the concept feel immediate and playful instead of academic. The line between proof and game got delightfully thin.
